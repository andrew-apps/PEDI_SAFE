# When to Call the Pediatrician About Fever

## EMERGENCY - Call 911 or Go to ER Immediately

- Child is having difficulty breathing or breathing very fast
- Child has a seizure (convulsion)
- Child is unresponsive or very difficult to wake
- Child has a stiff neck
- Child has purple or blood-colored spots on skin
- Child appears very ill, confused, or delirious

## URGENT - Call Pediatrician Right Away

### Based on Age
- **Under 3 months**: ANY fever of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher (rectal)
- **3-6 months**: Fever of 101°F (38.3°C) or higher
- **6-12 months**: Fever of 102°F (38.9°C) or higher
- **Any age**: Fever above 104°F (40°C)

### Based on Symptoms
- Fever lasting more than 24 hours in child under 2 years
- Fever lasting more than 3 days in child of any age
- Fever returns after being gone for 24+ hours
- Child refuses to drink or cannot keep fluids down
- Signs of dehydration: no tears, dry mouth, no wet diapers in 8+ hours
- Child has severe headache
- Child has unusual skin rash
- Child has repeated vomiting or diarrhea
- Child is crying inconsolably
- Child appears listless or unusually sleepy
- Child has burning or pain during urination

## Special Circumstances - Always Call

- Child has immune system problems or chronic illness
- Child is on immunosuppressive medications
- Child has sickle cell disease
- Child recently had surgery
- Child recently received vaccines (fever usually mild, but call if concerned)
- Child has traveled recently to areas with infectious diseases

## When Home Care May Be Appropriate

- Child is over 3 months with low-grade fever (under 102°F/38.9°C)
- Child is drinking well and urinating normally
- Child is alert and playing during fever-free periods
- Fever has lasted less than 3 days
- No other concerning symptoms
- Child responds well to comfort measures

## Important Notes

- The behavior of your child is more important than the temperature reading
- A very high fever does not necessarily mean a serious illness
- A lower fever in a very sick-appearing child is more concerning
- Trust your instincts - if something seems wrong, call your doctor
- When in doubt, always call your pediatrician
